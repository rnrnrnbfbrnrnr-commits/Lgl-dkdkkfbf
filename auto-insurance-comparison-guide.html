How to Compare Auto Insurance Quotes Without Getting Played

RN
By R. Novak, Editorial Team
· 8 min read · Updated July 2026

Two auto insurance quotes with the same monthly premium can offer very different protection. Insurers know that most shoppers compare on price alone, so the fastest way to lower a quote is to quietly lower the limits behind it. Here's how to compare quotes the way an underwriter would.

Start with liability limits, not the total price

Liability coverage is usually written as three numbers, such as 100/300/100 — meaning $100,000 per person and $300,000 per accident for bodily injury, plus $100,000 for property damage. A quote with 25/50/25 limits will always look cheaper than one with 100/300/100, but it leaves you personally responsible for any costs above those limits after a serious accident. Line up the liability numbers first, before looking at price at all.

Check the deductible on every coverage separately

Collision and comprehensive coverage each carry their own deductible, and they don't have to match. A quote can look cheaper simply because it quietly raised the comprehensive deductible from $500 to $1,000. Ask for the deductible on each coverage type in writing before comparing totals.

Worth knowing: Comprehensive covers non-collision events — theft, weather, animal strikes, vandalism. Collision covers impacts with another vehicle or object. A policy can include one without the other, which matters if you're financing or leasing the car, since lenders typically require both.

Uninsured and underinsured motorist coverage

This coverage pays when the at-fault driver has no insurance, or not enough to cover your costs — a real risk, since a meaningful share of drivers on the road carry no coverage at all depending on the state. It's often sold as an option rather than bundled by default, and it's one of the first things cut to lower a quote. For the cost involved, it's rarely worth dropping.

Add-ons that change the real comparison

  • Gap insurance — pays the difference between what you owe on a loan and the car's actual cash value after a total loss. Matters most in the first two years of a loan or lease.
  • Rental reimbursement — covers a rental car while yours is being repaired after a covered claim. Without it, you're paying out of pocket during the repair window.
  • Roadside assistance — towing, jump-starts, lockouts. Often cheaper through a standalone membership than as a policy add-on.
  • New car replacement — replaces a totaled newer vehicle with a new one, rather than paying only its depreciated value.

A five-line comparison method

Before comparing total price, write down five numbers for each quote: bodily injury per-person limit, bodily injury per-accident limit, property damage limit, collision deductible, and comprehensive deductible. Only compare final prices once all five numbers match across quotes. If an insurer won't hold those numbers steady while you shop, that quote isn't comparable to the others yet — it's a different product wearing the same price tag.

What tends to get missed

  1. Mileage estimates that don't match actual driving — usage-based adjustments can change renewal pricing significantly.
  2. Lapses in prior coverage, which insurers treat as a risk signal even when the gap was involuntary.
  3. Named-driver exclusions buried in the policy, which can void coverage if that excluded person is driving at the time of a claim.
